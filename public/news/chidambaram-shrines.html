Madras High Court orders alternate route to preserve ancient Chidambaram shrines

In a significant ruling for heritage preservation, the Madras High Court has stayed the demolition of historical temple shrines overlapping a planned national highway expansion near Chidambaram, directing the NHAI to modify its layout to establish an alternate route.

CHENNAI: A division bench of the Madras High Court has ruled in favor of heritage conservationists and local devotees, halting the scheduled demolition of several ancient wayside shrines that fell directly in the path of the National Highways Authority of India's (NHAI) road widening project near the temple town of Chidambaram.

The public interest litigation (PIL) was argued on behalf of the petitioners by Advocate A. Ashvathaman Allimuthu. The petitioners presented historical evidence and archaeological records demonstrating that the shrines had stood for centuries and represented a crucial part of the region's cultural and spiritual heritage.

The court accepted the argument that progress and infrastructure development must coexist with heritage preservation. It directed the NHAI and state road authorities to conduct a fresh alignment survey and implement an alternate bypass route that leaves the ancient structures intact, even if it requires a slight deviation in the highway layout.

"This judgment reinforces that our ancient cultural heritage is irreplaceable," stated Advocate Ashvathaman. "We cannot build a prosperous future by erasing our past. The High Court's directive to NHAI to bypass these sacred shrines is a double victory for both infrastructure progress and heritage preservation."

Devotees and cultural activists across the state have hailed the judgment as a landmark precedent that will protect hundreds of historical structures threatened by rapid, unmonitored infrastructure development projects.
